    Unit 5 Christmas every day: 'Have to' and 'must'

    Last Christmas, I received a gift from my friend. It is a sweater. It fits me very well and transfers me to a fashionable person. I am so happy and that's why I wish we could be at Christmas every day.

    By the way, did you have a big meal with your family on Christmas Day? Listen to the following podcast and think over whether you 'have to' or 'must' lose weight after Christmas.

    If you have to do something, it is necessary for you to do it. It’s a law, an obligation or a fact. For instance, you have to get a visa if you want to go to Russia. If you must do something, it is necessary for you to do it, but this is often your opinion or a rule that you have made yourself. For instance, I must remember to buy a birthday gift for my mother. We see must (not have to) in formal written notices or rules that an organization has made itself. Here is an example: All students must be at the examination hall fifteen minutes before the exam.

    We can often use either have to or must in a sentence. But be careful! There a big difference in meaning between don’t have to and mustn’t.If you don’t have to do something, it isn’t necessary to do it, but you can if you want. If you mustn’t do something, it means ‘don’t do it’. It is necessary not to do it. See the differences:

    • You don’t have to finish the spreadsheet today. Tomorrow will be fine.
    • Employees mustn’t send passwords by email.

    It is possible to ask questions with Must + subject...? but it is much more common to use Do/Does + subject + have to...? And there is no future or past form of must. We use will have to (future)and had to (past).

    • Do I have to come to the meeting tomorrow? (Must I come to the meeting tomorrow?)
    • It’s too late now. You’ll have to talk toher tomorrow.
    • All the trains were cancelled, so we had to get a taxi.

    In informal British English, we sometimes use have got to instead of have to. They mean the same. For instance, You’ve got to get a visa if you want to go to Russia.

    Take note: Have to and must are modal verbs. When we say modal verbs, we usually say them quickly and often without emphasis. So have to sounds like ‘hafta’(haf tə). Must sounds like ‘mus’ (məs). You don’t hear the final /t/ sound in must.

    Vocabularies: binomials
    Yes, in the following podcast, you would learn some short and sweet phrases English speakers love to use in everyday English. Sick and tired is a phrase which means annoyed and bored. Phrases like this with two nouns joined by and are very common and useful in English. They're called binomials.

    • aches and pains: general body pains that aren’t serious
    • cheap and cheerful: simple, basic and often inexpensive
    • short and sweet: quick and simple
    • black and blue: sore and bruised
    • quick and dirty: short, simple and basic

    Unit 6: Great achievers

    The theme of this unit is about 'past simple' tense in English. It is quite easy to understand. But I still learned something new, which is the pronunciation related to the past simple tense:

    • /Id/ For verbs that end in /t/ or /d/ sounds: decided, waited;
    • /t/ For verbs that end in /p/, /k/, /sh/ or /ch/ sounds: stopped, finished;
    • /d/ For most other verbs: lived, loved.

    Both –ing and –ed adjectives help us to talk about our feelings.The main difference is that –ing adjectives are usually used to talk about the person, place or thing that is making us feel a certain way:

    • Yawn! This lecture is really boring!
    But –ed adjectives are used to talk about how we feel:
    • I’ve been listening to him for hours – I’m so bored.
    Be careful: If you say I am boring – it’s grammatically correct, but it means that you make other people feel bored!
    Hope now you know the differences between those paris of words: relaxing and relaxed; confusing and confused.

    Unit 7: The Titanic (Past simple and past continuous)

    Watch this video, and learn when you should use past continuous tense.

    We use the past continuous to talk about the past:

    • for something which happened before and after another action: The children were doing their homework when I got home.
    • for something that happened before and after a specific time: It was eight o'clock. I was writing a letter.
    • to show that something continued for some time: Everyone was shouting.
    • for something that happened again and again: I was practising every day, three times a day.
    • with verbs which show change or growth:The children were growing up quickly.
    Take note:verbs we don’t use in the past continuous. There are some verbs that we don’t usually use in the continuous form. They are often verbs related to the senses and thinking, for example: hear, see, smell, hate, know, understand, believe, notice, want, need, seem, wish. For instance, Did you know Jess when you were living in Madrid?
