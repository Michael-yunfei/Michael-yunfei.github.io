Inner Product Space

10pm, 25, Nov 2020

This week, I am studying Fourier transform and distributions. It takes me days to understand the relation between Fourier transform and distributions. Like most students, I studied how to do Fourier transform with the following formula: $$\begin{align}\hat{f}(s) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) e^{-2\pi i s x} d x , \ \ \ s \in \mathbb{R}\end{align}$$ I view Fourier transform as the continuous counterpart of Fourier coefficient: $$c_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_{a}^b f(x) e^{-2\pi i n x/L} dx, \ \ \ n \in \mathbb{Z}$$ According to Prof. Brad Osgood from Standford University, there is nothing wrong to understand Fourier transform in this way. However, I am complete lost later when he introduced concept of tempered distribution, which is a continuous linear function on a space of test functions (Osgood, 2008 Online).

Trying to get the gist of tempered distribution, I did some research by searching and reading more notes. Thanks to the thesis written by Terri Joan Harris (2015), I come to realize that tempered distribution is a subject of functional analysis, which is concerned with infinite-dimensional spaces. Complete normed vector spaces over the real or complex numbers, called Banach spaces, are the foundation of this field of study. An important example is the Hilbert space where the norm arises from an inner product.

Before talking about inner product space, let's review dot product first. For \(x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n\), the dot product of \(x\) and \(y\) is defined by $$x \cdot y = x_1 y_1 + x_2 y_2 + \cdots + x_n y_n$$, where \(x = (x_1, x_2, \cdots x_n) \) and \(y = (y_1, y_2, \cdots y_n) \). For \(y \in \mathbb{R}^n \) fixed, the map from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) to \(\mathbb{R}\) is linear: $$f(x) \to x \cdot y $$ as $$\begin{align} f(u+v) & = (u + v) \cdot y = u \cdot y + v \cdot y = f(u) + f(v)\\ f(\alpha u) & = \alpha f(u)\end{align}$$. An inner product space is a generalization of the dot product. It is defined by abstracting the properties of the dot product in real vectors. However, we want to make it more general by defining inner product space in both real and compelx vector space.

Before making this definition, we need examine the complex numbers case first. Recall that if \(\lambda = a + b i\) where \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\), then

  • \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{(a^2 + b^2)} \)
  • The complex conjugate of \(\lambda\) is \(\bar{\lambda} = a - bi \)
  • \(|\lambda|^2 = \lambda \bar{\lambda} \)
For \(z = (z_1, z_2, \cdots, z_n) \in \mathbb{C}^n \), we define the norm of \(z\) by $$||z||= \sqrt{|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 + \cdots + |z_n|^2}$$ Note that $$||z||^2 = z_1 \overline{z_1} + \cdots + z_n \overline{z_n}$$

The above equation suggests that the inner product of \(w=(w_1, w_2, \cdots , w_n) \) with \(z\) should be $$w \cdot z = w_1 \overline{z_1} + w_1 \overline{z_2} + \cdots + w_n \overline{z_n}$$ If the roles of \(w and z\) interchanged, then we could replace above equation with their conjugate elements. Now, we are ready to define define inner product space.

Definition: inner product
An innder product on \(V\) is a fucntion that takes each ordered pair \( (u, v) \) of elements of \(V\) to a number \( \langle u, v\rangle \in F \) and has the following properties:

  • positivity: \( \langle v, v \rangle \geq 0 \) for all \( v \in V \);
  • definiteness: \( \langle v, v \rangle = 0 \) if and only if \(v = 0\);
  • aditivity in first slot: \( \langle u + v, w \rangle = \langle u, w \rangle + \langle v, w \rangle \)
  • homogeneity in first slow: \( \langle \lambda u, v \rangle = \lambda \langle u, v \rangle \) for all \(\lambda \in F \) and all \(u, v \in V \)
  • conjugate symmetry: \( \langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle} \) for all \(u, v \in V \).

Examples
The Euclidean inner product on \(F^n\) is defined by $$\langle(w_1, \cdots, w_n), (z_1, \cdots, z_n) \rangle = w_1 \overline{z_1} + \cdots + w_n\overline{z_n}$$
An inner product can be defined on the vector space of continuous real-valued functions on the interval \([-1, 1]\) by $$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_{-1}^1 f(x) g(x) dx$$ One should verify this equation satisfies those five properties defined in the above definition.

Definition: vector space
A vector space is a set \(V\) along with an addition on \(V\) and a scalar multiplication on \(V\) such that the following properties hold:

  • commutativity: \( u + v = v + u \) for all \(u, v \in V\);
  • associativity: \( (u+v) + w = u + (v + w) \) and \( (ab) v = a(bv) \) for all \(u, v, w \in V \) and all \(a, b \in F \);
  • additive identity: there exsits an element \(0 \in V \) such that \(v + 0 = v \) for all \( v \in V \);
  • additive inverse: for every \( v \in V \), there exists \(w \in V \) such that \(v + w = 0 \);
  • multiplicative identity: \(1 v = v \) for all \(v \in V \);
  • distributive properties: \( a ( u + v) = a u + av \) and \( ( a + b) v = av + bv \) for all \(a , b \in F \) and all \(u, v \in V \).

Definition: inner product space
An inner product space is a vector space \(V \) along with an inner product on \(V \).

The most important example of an inner product space is \( F^n \) with the Euclidean inner product. Now, let's study basic properties of an inner product:

  1. For each fixed \(u \in V\), the function that takes \(v\) to \( \langle v, u \rangle \) is a linear map from \( V \) to \(F \)
  2. \( \langle u, 0 \rangle = \langle 0, u \rangle = 0 \) for every \( u \in V \)
  3. \( \langle u, v + w \rangle = \langle u, v \rangle + \langle u, w \rangle\) for all \(u, v, w \in V \).
  4. \( \langle u, \lambda v \rangle = \bar{\lambda} \langle u, v \rangle \) for all \(\lambda \in F \) and \(u, v \in V \).

Definition: norm, \( ||v || \)
For \(v \in V \), the norm of \(v\), denoted \( ||v || \), is defined by $$||v|| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$$. For example, in the vector space of continuous real-value functions on \([-1, 1]\), we have $$\begin{equation}||f|| = \sqrt{\int_{-1}^1 (f(x))^2 dx} \end{equation}$$. Suppose \(v \in V \), we could have:

  1. \(||v || = 0 \) if and only if \( v = 0 \).
  2. \( || \lambda v || = |\lambda | ||v || \) for all \( \lambda \in F \).
