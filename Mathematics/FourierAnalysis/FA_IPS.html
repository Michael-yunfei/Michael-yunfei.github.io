Inner Product Space

Before introducing innder product space, it is helpful to review hierarchy of mathematical spaces presented in the following figure.


Since topological spaces are the most abstract one, we will not discuss it at the moment. Let's start to talk about metric spaces first, which most students (include me) are quite familiar with. In mathematics, a metric space is a set together with a metric on the set. The metric is a function that defines a concept of distance between any two members of the set.

Definition
A metric space \( (\mathcal{M}, d) \) is a set \( \mathcal{M} \) together with a function \( d: \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R} \) called a metric satisfying four conditions:

  1. \(d (x, y ) \geq 0 \) for all \(x, y \in \mathcal{M} \). (non-negativeness)
  2. \( d(x, y) = 0 \) if and only if \( x = y \). (identification)
  3. \(d (x, y) = d(y, x) \) for all \(x, y \in \mathcal{M} \). (symmetry)
  4. \(d (x, y) \leq d (x, z) + d(z, y) \) for all \(x, y, z \in \mathcal{M} \). (triangle inequality)
For example, the typical distance used in the Euclidean space is defined as $$d(x, y) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - y_i)^2}$$ In general, one could define many distance functions, such as $$d(x, y) \equiv \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i - y_i|$$

Very often, we are interested in a subset of functions called norms. We will define them in a way that it applies to any vector. Therefore, it is very natural for us now to introduce vector space.

Definition
A vector space is a set \(V\) along with an addition on \(V\) and a scalar multiplication on \(V\) such that the following properties hold:

  • commutativity: \( u + v = v + u \) for all \(u, v \in V\);
  • associativity: \( (u+v) + w = u + (v + w) \) and \( (ab) v = a(bv) \) for all \(u, v, w \in V \) and all \(a, b \in F \);
  • additive identity: there exsits an element \(0 \in V \) such that \(v + 0 = v \) for all \( v \in V \);
  • additive inverse: for every \( v \in V \), there exists \(w \in V \) such that \(v + w = 0 \);
  • multiplicative identity: \(1 v = v \) for all \(v \in V \);
  • distributive properties: \( a ( u + v) = a u + av \) and \( ( a + b) v = av + bv \) for all \(a , b \in F \) and all \(u, v \in V \).
Definition
Let \( \mathcal{V} \) be a vector space (real or complex), and function \( N : \mathcal{V} \to \mathbb{R} \) (\(\mathbb{C} \) if it was in complex vector space). We say \( N (\cdot) \) is a norm if the following conditions hold:
  1. \(N (v) \geq 0 \) for all \(v \in \mathcal{V} \).
  2. \(N (v) = 0 \) if and only if \(v = 0\).
  3. \(N (\lambda v) = |\lambda | N(v) \) for all \(v \in \mathcal{V} \) and \( \lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) (\(\mathbb{C}\) if it was in complex vector space ).
  4. \( N(v + w) \leq N(v) + N(w) \) for all \(v, w \in \mathcal{V} \).
When we have a normed vector space (\(\mathcal{V}, N \)), we can obtain a metric on \(\mathcal{V} \) by setting \(d(x, y) = N(x -y) \) for all \(x, y \in \mathcal{V} \).

Definition
An inner product space is a vector space \( \mathcal{V} \) over the field \( \mathbb{F}\) (either the field of real numbers \(\mathbb{R}\) or the field of complex numbers \(\mathbb{C}\)) together with a map: $$\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V} \to \mathbb{F}$$ called an inner product that satisfies the following conditions:

  • positivity: \( \langle v, v \rangle \geq 0 \) for all \( v \in V \);
  • definiteness: \( \langle v, v \rangle = 0 \) if and only if \(v = 0\);
  • aditivity in first slot: \( \langle u + v, w \rangle = \langle u, w \rangle + \langle v, w \rangle \)
  • homogeneity in first slow: \( \langle \lambda u, v \rangle = \lambda \langle u, v \rangle \) for all \(\lambda \in F \) and all \(u, v \in V \)
  • conjugate symmetry: \( \langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle} \) for all \(u, v \in V \).

Examples
The Euclidean inner product on \(F^n\) is defined by $$\langle(w_1, \cdots, w_n), (z_1, \cdots, z_n) \rangle = w_1 \overline{z_1} + \cdots + w_n\overline{z_n}$$ Therefore, the real n-space \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the dot product is an inner product space. An inner product can be defined on the vector space of continuous real-valued functions on the interval \([-1, 1]\) by $$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_{-1}^1 f(x) \overline{g(x)} dx$$ One should verify this equation satisfies those five properties defined in the above definition when we put this dot product into the complex linear space.
